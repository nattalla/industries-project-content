# Real Estate Private Equity
## Organizational Strategy for Asset Performance

---

### Industry Context
*Current market dynamics and organizational pressures*

Real Estate Private Equity is emerging from one of its most challenging periods in recent history. After values declined 12.5% from peak through 2023, the sector showed signs of stabilization in 2024 with global real estate deal value growing 11% to $707 billion—the first increase in three years. This partial rebound was driven by rate cuts creating more favorable financing, cap rate compression, and reduced supply in sectors like multifamily and industrial. Yet fundraising remains severely constrained, falling 28% to $104 billion in 2024—the lowest level since 2012.

The industry faces a fundamental reset. The era of easy returns through financial engineering and market appreciation has ended. Returns for closed-end real estate funds remained negative through Q3 2024 with a pooled IRR of -1.1%. Open-end funds recorded their second annual decline since the global financial crisis. Many firms are holding assets longer than planned as the exit market remains challenged despite improvements. The median holding period reached seven years in 2023 before declining to 5.9 years in 2024—still well above historic averages.

Yet within this difficult environment, opportunity exists for firms with genuine operational capabilities. The highest-performing real estate investors are those with operational expertise taking market share from pure capital allocators. GPs with operational capabilities accounted for 37% of real estate assets under management in 2023, up 11 percentage points over the past decade. As one industry leader noted, firms with the ability to manage tenant relationships, optimize property operations, and drive NOI growth through hands-on management are outperforming those focused primarily on financial structuring.

Sector performance varies dramatically. Office remains the most challenged, particularly in the U.S., with fundamental questions about long-term demand post-pandemic. Residential, industrial, and logistics are recovering with improved fundamentals. Alternative sectors including manufactured housing, senior housing, and data centers are posting robust returns. Life sciences, student housing, and self-storage continue attracting capital. The dispersion in performance across property types and geographies is wider than at any time in the past decade.

The 2025 environment presents both challenge and opportunity. Interest rates are stabilizing, creating more predictable underwriting. Approximately $1.5 trillion of commercial real estate debt matures by end of 2025, creating refinancing pressure but also distressed opportunities. Construction starts have slowed across most sectors, improving future supply-demand dynamics. But success requires more than capital deployment—it demands operational excellence in property management, value-add execution, tenant relationship management, and asset-level performance optimization that many financial sponsors haven't built.

### Common Operational Challenges
*Where property-level performance meets organizational execution*

**Portfolio Asset Management at Scale**
Your fund manages 50+ properties across multifamily, industrial, office, and retail sectors. Each asset has different management teams, different operational challenges, different value creation opportunities. But your investment professionals are spread thin monitoring financial performance across the portfolio without deep operational insight into what's actually happening at the property level. You're reacting to problems rather than proactively managing performance, and it's impossible to identify best practices from top-performing assets and replicate them across the portfolio.

**Value-Add Execution That Doesn't Add Value**
You underwrite acquisitions with detailed value-add business plans—renovations to increase rents, operational improvements to boost NOI, repositioning to attract higher-quality tenants. On paper, these plans show attractive returns. But 18 months post-acquisition, execution is behind schedule, over budget, and not delivering projected rent growth. Why? Because investment professionals who structured the deal don't have construction management expertise, property management experience, or leasing capabilities to drive execution. The gap between investment thesis and operational reality is destroying returns.

**Third-Party Management Relationships That Don't Create Accountability**
Most of your properties are managed by third-party property management companies. You're paying them fees, but it's unclear what value they're creating. Occupancy could be higher, operating expenses could be lower, tenant satisfaction could improve. But you don't have the expertise to evaluate property management performance or the leverage to demand better results. The management agreements were negotiated during acquisition and haven't been renegotiated since, even as asset strategy has evolved.

**Capital Deployment Pressure vs. Disciplined Underwriting**
Your fund raised $800M with a four-year investment period. Three years in, you've deployed only 60% of committed capital. LP pressure to deploy capital is intense, but attractive deals meeting your investment criteria are scarce. You're stuck between pressure to put capital to work and the discipline required to avoid bad investments. This tension is creating implicit pressure on investment committees to approve marginal deals that would have been rejected in less pressured environments.

**Exit Readiness as a Scramble**
Your fund is approaching its exit window with multiple assets that need to be sold in the next 18-24 months. But the properties aren't prepared for sale—deferred capital expenditures need to be addressed, tenant rosters need improvement, financial reporting needs to be cleaned up. You're scrambling to get assets exit-ready, spending capital that hurts IRR, and discovering organizational weaknesses that buyers will identify during diligence. The properties should have been managed for exit from day one, but that didn't happen.

**Sector Specialization vs. Platform Efficiency**
Your fund pursues opportunistic real estate across multiple sectors—multifamily, industrial, retail, office. This diversity provides flexibility and risk mitigation. But each sector requires specialized expertise: multifamily operations are completely different from industrial asset management. Your platform isn't large enough to support dedicated teams for each sector, so investment professionals are generalists trying to manage specialized assets. The result is mediocre performance across sectors rather than excellence in any.

### Where Executives Get Stuck
*The invisible handbrakes on real estate performance*

**The Illusion of Property Management**
Here's what we see repeatedly: A real estate PE firm acquires an asset and keeps the existing property management in place. Why change what's working, right? The problem is that "working" is defined narrowly as keeping the property leased and operational. But is management maximizing rent growth? Optimizing operating expenses? Proactively renewing tenants? Identifying capital improvement opportunities? Most firms don't have the expertise to evaluate property management quality or the capabilities to improve it. They're paying management fees for mediocre performance and don't realize it until they compare their assets to best-in-class competitors.

**Financial Sponsor Mentality in an Operating Business**
Real estate PE firms are often staffed by people from investment banking, traditional private equity, or finance backgrounds. They're excellent at deal execution, financing strategies, and capital structure. But real estate is an operating business—value is created at the property level through tenant relationships, operational efficiency, capital improvement execution, and market positioning. The financial sponsor mentality treats properties as financial assets rather than operating businesses requiring hands-on management. This disconnect becomes expensive when value-add business plans require operational execution that the team can't deliver.

**Value-Add as Financial Engineering, Not Operations**
Many firms underwrite value-add opportunities as financial exercises: "If we spend $5M on renovations, rents will increase 20%, and we'll generate a 25% IRR." But value-add is operational, not financial. Can you actually execute renovations on time and budget while maintaining occupancy? Can you successfully lease the renovated units at projected rents? Do you have the relationships with contractors, architects, and leasing agents to make it happen? Underwriting assumes execution capability that may not exist, and the IRR models don't reflect operational reality.

**The Office Sector Existential Crisis**
Office real estate fundamentally changed during the pandemic, but many firms are still underwriting office properties using pre-2020 assumptions about occupancy, tenant demand, and building requirements. Trophy assets with top-tier tenants in prime locations may recover, but Class B office in secondary markets faces structural demand challenges. Yet firms are stuck holding office assets they can't sell at acceptable prices and can't reposition without massive capital. The longer they hold hoping for recovery, the more value they lose. This is an organizational capability problem as much as a market problem—most firms don't have expertise to reposition office assets for alternative uses (life sciences, residential conversion, data centers).

**Talent Gap Between Investment and Operations**
Real estate PE firms hire people who can underwrite deals and structure transactions. These are different skills than managing property operations, executing capital improvements, or optimizing tenant relationships. The talent profile needed for investment success is different from the talent profile needed for operational excellence. Firms recognize this gap but struggle to bridge it—operational experts don't fit naturally into investment teams, compensation structures differ, and career paths aren't clear. Without solving the talent integration challenge, firms can't build genuine operational capabilities.

*Building operational excellence that creates property-level value*

We map how information flows between investment professionals, asset managers, property management, and external partners. This reveals whether your organization enables the coordination required for successful value-add execution.

For a $1.5B real estate fund that acquired properties across multiple sectors, ONA showed that investment professionals who underwrote deals had minimal ongoing communication with asset managers responsible for execution. Value-add business plans were developed during acquisition but not effectively communicated to the asset management team. As a result, execution priorities didn't match investment thesis, capital improvement timing was suboptimal, and operating expense reduction initiatives were never implemented. We helped them redesign coordination structures that kept investment and asset management aligned throughout hold periods, and value-add execution success rates improved dramatically.

We worked with a multi-sector real estate platform where each sector team (multifamily, industrial, retail) operated independently with no cross-sector knowledge sharing. The industrial team had developed excellent vendor relationships and capital improvement processes, but the multifamily team was reinventing similar solutions. ONA revealed exactly where knowledge silos existed and which relationships needed building. The resulting collaboration enabled platform-wide operational excellence rather than sector-specific pockets of competence.

#### Organizational Change Management (OCM)
Real estate PE firms face constant transformation: implementing new property management systems, integrating acquisitions, shifting strategies (office to industrial), or building operational capabilities. We build change capabilities that enable transformation without disrupting asset performance.

A value-add multifamily fund needed to standardize operations across 30 properties that had been acquired over three years. Each property had different property management, different systems, different processes. Standardization was necessary for operational efficiency and exit preparation, but the change would affect dozens of property management employees who'd never worked together. We designed the change management approach that built buy-in for standardization, provided training on new systems and processes, and created communication that helped property-level teams understand how standardization supported their success. The standardization succeeded without operational disruptions, occupancy remained stable through transition, and the portfolio became materially more valuable due to operational consistency.

For firms shifting sector focus (e.g., office-heavy funds pivoting to industrial and multifamily), we manage the organizational implications. Sector pivots require different expertise, different vendor relationships, different operational approaches. We help firms make strategic shifts without losing institutional knowledge or creating organizational chaos.

#### Business Process Engineering (BPE)
Sustainable performance in real estate PE requires operational processes that create property-level value consistently across diverse assets. We engineer capital improvement workflows, property management oversight procedures, and performance monitoring frameworks that scale.

A value-add residential fund was managing renovation programs across 20 multifamily properties simultaneously. Each property was executing renovations differently with different contractors, different project management approaches, different unit turnover processes. There was no standardization, no sharing of best practices, no portfolio-level visibility into renovation performance. We mapped the end-to-end capital improvement workflow and created standardized processes that maintained property-specific flexibility while ensuring consistent execution. We built performance tracking that identified which properties were executing most effectively and why. Renovation cycle times decreased 30%, cost per unit renovated dropped 15%, and rent achievement on renovated units exceeded projections by 12%.

When a diversified real estate fund needed to create consistent asset management practices across sectors, we engineered the unified framework that balanced sector-specific requirements with consistent value creation methodology. The framework improved asset performance, enabled meaningful portfolio-level analysis, and created knowledge transfer across properties that drove continuous improvement.

#### Organizational Development & Effectiveness (OD&E)
Real estate PE success requires leadership teams that think both financially and operationally—underwriting deals while understanding property operations, capital improvements, and tenant dynamics. We develop leadership capability and organizational culture that supports operational value creation.

A real estate PE fund had excellent deal execution capabilities but weak asset management. Investment professionals were skilled financial analysts who couldn't evaluate property management quality, oversee construction execution, or drive operational improvements. We didn't replace them—we developed their operational fluency. We created learning experiences with property management experts, facilitated property tours that taught investment professionals what excellent operations looked like, and built frameworks that helped them think operationally during underwriting. Over 18 months, the investment team developed genuine operational insight that improved both acquisition decisions and asset management.

For real estate firms building new sector expertise (e.g., traditional multifamily investors adding industrial or life sciences), we accelerate capability development through targeted hiring, strategic property management partnerships, and knowledge transfer programs that build organizational expertise faster than trial and error would allow.

### Common Scenarios Where Rooted Adds Value
*Real situations where organizational strategy drives real estate returns*

**Building Value-Add Execution Capabilities**
A $900M value-add multifamily fund had strong acquisition execution—they won competitive deals through relationship sourcing and creative structuring. But post-acquisition value-add execution was inconsistent. Some properties successfully completed renovations on time and budget with strong rent achievement. Others were over budget, behind schedule, with disappointing leasing results. The variability in execution created material IRR dispersion across the portfolio that couldn't be explained by market differences.

We conducted operational assessment and discovered the fund lacked standardized value-add execution capabilities. Each property executed renovations differently based on local property management discretion. There was no renovation playbook, no vendor standards, no project management framework. Success depended on whether individual property managers happened to be good at managing construction. We built the value-add execution infrastructure: created standardized renovation specifications, established approved vendor networks, designed project management workflows, and built performance tracking that identified execution problems early. We trained property management teams on the new approach and created knowledge-sharing forums where successful properties taught struggling ones. Within 18 months, renovation execution became consistent across the portfolio, and IRR dispersion decreased materially.

**Professionalizing Asset Management Across Multi-Sector Portfolio**
A $1.2B opportunistic real estate fund invested across multifamily, industrial, retail, and office. This diversity provided risk mitigation and deal flexibility. But asset management was fragmented—different approaches for different sectors, inconsistent reporting, no cross-sector learning. Investment professionals were spread thin trying to monitor assets in sectors they didn't understand deeply. The fund's LPs were asking increasingly sophisticated questions about asset-level performance that the firm couldn't answer confidently.

We designed the professional asset management function appropriate for a multi-sector platform. We created sector specialists within asset management who developed deep expertise in their domains, established standardized reporting that enabled portfolio-level analysis while respecting sector differences, and built knowledge transfer mechanisms that spread best practices across properties. We implemented performance monitoring systems that gave investment professionals real-time visibility into asset performance without requiring them to become sector experts. Most importantly, we helped the firm create the organizational structure that balanced sector specialization with platform efficiency. Asset performance improved across sectors, LP confidence increased, and the firm successfully raised its next fund with LP enthusiasm for the firm's asset management capabilities.

**Preparing Portfolio for Strategic Sale Process**
A real estate fund approached its exit window with 12 properties that needed to be sold over 18 months. But the properties weren't ready for institutional buyer diligence. Financial reporting was inconsistent across properties, deferred maintenance had accumulated, tenant rosters had weaknesses, and capital improvement records were incomplete. The fund needed to get properties exit-ready quickly without destroying value through rushed spending.

We designed the exit preparation program. We conducted operational assessments of each property identifying specific improvements needed for optimal sale positioning, prioritized capital improvements that would enhance value versus those that could be deferred, helped the fund negotiate with current property management to improve operations where possible versus transitioning to new management where necessary, and created standardized reporting that made buyer diligence efficient. We coordinated across properties to execute improvements systematically rather than reactively. Most importantly, we helped leadership think strategically about which properties to sell first, which to hold for better market conditions, and how to package assets to maximize total portfolio value. The exit program succeeded: properties sold at valuations exceeding initial projections, buyer diligence was smooth, and the fund returned capital to LPs ahead of schedule.

**Building Operational Capabilities for Office-to-Alternative Use Repositioning**
A real estate fund held several Class B office assets that were fundamentally impaired by post-pandemic demand shifts. Waiting for office recovery wasn't a strategy; the fund needed to reposition assets for alternative uses—residential conversion, life sciences, mixed-use redevelopment. But the investment team had no experience with complex repositioning requiring entitlements, design, construction, and completely different tenant bases.

We helped them build repositioning capabilities. We facilitated partnerships with specialized operating partners who brought expertise in residential conversion and life sciences development. We created the governance frameworks that balanced fund control with operator expertise. We designed decision-making processes for repositioning that integrated operator input with investment committee oversight. We helped the team build the evaluation framework for assessing which assets were candidates for which repositioning strategies. Three properties underwent successful repositioning—two office-to-residential conversions and one office-to-life sciences—creating value that salvaged what would have been impaired assets. The repositioning capabilities became a competitive advantage that allowed the fund to acquire distressed office assets from sellers unable to execute repositioning themselves.
